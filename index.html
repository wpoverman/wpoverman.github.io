Bio

My research develops tools for analyzing large-scale social data, aiming to provide a better understanding of social structure and behaviors online while also impacting the design of digital social systems. My recent work has focused on social media algorithms [ArXiv'24, JOTS'23], reducing political polarization online [ICWSM'22a, ICWSM'22b, WSDM'21, WWW'18], improving the health of online conversations [WWW'21, CSCW'21], and causal inference in social systems [ICML'24, NeurIPS'23, KDD'17, Biometrika'19]. My research often falls at the intersections of Social Networks, Machine Learning, and Causal Inference.

My research focuses on developing theoretically grounded approaches to AI safety and alignment, with a particular emphasis on AI control and scalable oversight. I'm especially interested in how tools from black-box uncertainty quantification, reinforcement learning, and theoretical computer science can help us reason rigorously about the behavior of powerful AI systems.

I'm currently a Ph.D. student in the Operations, Information, and Technology group at Stanford University, advised by Mohsen Bayati. Prior to Stanford I was a visiting researcher in South Korea, durign which time I concurrently obtained (primarily virtually due to COVID) a M.Sc. in Computer Scince from UC Irvine. I graduated with a B.Sc. from Caltech in 2020, where I double majored in Mathematics and Computer Science.

Beyond AI safety and alignment, other areas of research during my graduate studies have been RL, causal inference, and AI applications in healthcare. Throughout my graduate studies, I have interned at Uber, where I have applied my research work in RL and causal inference to problems in the ridesharing domain. During my undergraduate studies at Caltech, I primarily focused on theoretical computer science and extremal combinatorics.
